## 1. Cartographic Style (Theatre Layer)

### Staff Maps
WWI-era military staff maps were utilitarian but beautiful by accident. Key characteristics:
- **Hand-drawn contour lines** — ink on linen or heavy paper, slightly irregular
- **Hachure shading** — short lines indicating slope direction, denser = steeper
- **Watercolour washes** — translucent colour indicating territory control, terrain type, or unit positions
- **Manuscript lettering** — place names in a mix of italic (water features), roman (settlements), and caps (regions). Size indicates importance.
- **Grid overlay** — thin red or blue lines for coordinate reference
- **Margin notes** — stamps, handwritten annotations, date marks. These add authenticity.

### Colour Vocabulary of Period Maps
- Territory control: blue (allied) and red (enemy) washes — never solid fills, always translucent
- Roads: double brown lines
- Railways: black lines with cross-hatching
- Water: blue ink, often with flow direction arrows
- Forests: green stipple or small tree symbols
- Elevation: brown contours or hachure

### Modern References
Games and media that have captured this style well:
- *Valiant Hearts* (Ubisoft) — hand-drawn WWI aesthetic, ink and wash
- *1917* opening map sequences — staff map aesthetic in film
- Ordnance Survey historical maps — the gold standard for cartographic craft

## 2. Scale Model & Diorama Aesthetics (Campaign Layer)

### Physical Model Characteristics
The campaign layer should evoke a museum-quality terrain model or wargaming table:
- **Material textures:** Plaster terrain, static grass, lichen bushes, painted surfaces with visible brushstrokes
- **Lighting:** Overhead, slightly warm — like a well-lit display case. Subtle shadows under terrain features.
- **Scale indicators:** The miniature quality should be felt. Trees are slightly too uniform. Buildings are simplified. The terrain has a crafted, not natural, quality.
- **Front line markers:** Physical objects — pins, thread, tape, small flags. These should look placed by hand, not generated.

### Damage and History
The terrain should show the passage of battle:
- **Cratering:** Shell craters in contested areas — small depressions with darker earth
- **Scorched ground:** Blackened, bare patches where the front has passed
- **Trench lines:** Visible as thin dark lines in heavily contested areas
- **Intact vs. damaged structures:** Buildings degrade as combat intensifies in their sector

### Miniature Wargaming Visual Language
The tabletop wargaming community has strong visual conventions:
- **Unit bases:** Coloured edges or flags indicating allegiance
- **Status markers:** Small tokens or rings indicating buffs/debuffs
- **Measurement sticks:** Could be evoked subtly for movement indicators
- **Dice:** The concept of visible randomness — the front-line roll results could briefly show as dice-like indicators before resolving

## 3. Newsreel & Period Photography (Action Layer)

### Authentic Film Characteristics
WWI-era film had distinctive qualities:
- **Frame rate:** Originally shot at 16-18fps, often projected at 24fps — creating the characteristic slightly-fast motion
- **Gate weave:** Subtle frame instability, more noticeable on static shots
- **Grain structure:** Coarse, visible, organic — not the fine grain of modern film. Especially visible in sky areas and flat tones.
- **Vignetting:** Darkened corners from simple lens optics
- **Focus:** Shallow depth of field, slight softness. Centre-sharp, edge-soft.
- **Exposure:** Highlights blow out easily. Shadows block up. Limited dynamic range compared to modern cameras.

### Hand-Tinting / Colourisation
Period colourisation was applied by hand, frame by frame:
- **Selective:** Not everything was coloured. Sky, uniforms, and fire were common targets. Backgrounds often left in sepia/grey.
- **Translucent:** Colour was applied as a wash over the monochrome base, not replacing it. The underlying tonal detail shows through.
- **Imprecise:** Colour doesn't perfectly follow object edges. Slight bleed and variation between frames adds to the hand-made quality.
- **Limited palette:** Typically 4-6 colours per scene. Blue (sky), red/orange (fire, insignia), green (foliage), yellow (sun/sand), flesh tone (faces visible in close-up).

### Intertitles
Silent film used title cards between scenes:
- **White text on black background** with decorative borders
- **Period typefaces** — often Art Nouveau or early grotesque sans-serif
- These could be used for dramatic moments: pilot introductions, mission briefings, kill confirmations

## 4. Period Typography

### Typeface Families Appropriate to the Era

**For cartographic/theatre use:**
- Garamond or Caslon — classic serif faces available in the period
- Hand-lettered styles — slightly irregular, mimicking cartographer's hand
- Copperplate Gothic — used on military documents and formal maps

**For military/campaign use:**
- Stencil faces — evoke military supply crates, field equipment
- Condensed grotesques — efficient, utilitarian, space-saving
- Typewriter faces (Courier, American Typewriter) — field dispatch feel

**For newsreel/action use:**
- Title card faces — bold serif or decorated sans-serif
- Typewriter — for overlaid text that should feel like field reports
- Minimal use — the action layer should avoid text where possible

### Typography Rules
- Never use modern geometric sans-serifs (Futura, Helvetica, Inter) in any layer — they break the period feel instantly
- Display type (titles, segment names) can be decorative; data type (numbers, stats) must prioritise legibility
- Numerals should use proportional figures for display, tabular figures for data columns
- Italic for place names on maps (cartographic convention)

## 5. Colour in WWI-Era Media

### The WWI Colour Palette
The war was experienced in muted, earthy tones:
- **Uniforms:** French horizon blue (#6B7F99), British khaki (#BDB298), German feldgrau (#4D5D53)
- **Landscape:** Flanders mud (#7B6B5A), Somme chalk (#D4C5A9), shell-crater water (#5E6B5E)
- **Sky:** Overcast grey (#B8B8B8), dawn rose (#D4A0A0), clear pale blue (#A8C4D8)
- **Fire and destruction:** Muzzle flash (#FFB347), burning structures (#CC5500), smoke (#787878)
- **Parchment and ink:** Paper (#F5E6D3), aged parchment (#E8D5B7), iron-gall ink (#2C1810), faded ink (#4A3728)

### Colour Psychology in This Context
- **Warm tones** (sepia, amber, parchment) = safety, planning, control. Use in theatre layer.
- **Neutral tones** (earth, mud, grey-green) = reality, consequence, ground truth. Use in campaign layer.
- **Cool/desaturated tones** (grey, pale blue, film grain) = memory, distance, the past. Use in action layer.
- **Red** = danger, loss, death — consistent across all layers, adapted to each palette.
- **Gold/amber** = achievement, medals, veteran status. Use sparingly for maximum impact.

### Saturation as a Design Tool
Saturation level communicates layer identity:
- Theatre: Low-medium saturation (watercolour washes)
- Campaign: Medium saturation (painted model)
- Action: Very low saturation with selective colour pops (hand-tinted footage)

A UI element moving between layers should shift its saturation to match. The resource colour for Manpower might be a warm brown on the theatre map, a slightly more vivid earth-red on the campaign overlay, and a muted rust in the action HUD.
