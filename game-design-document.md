# Game Design Document
## Working Title: *Skies of the Great War*
### Inspired by Ancient Art of War in the Skies (Evryware, 1992)

---

## Vision Statement

A three-layer WWI air strategy game for iPad that puts the player in command of an air force across an entire theatre of war. Abstract enough to be readable, deep enough to reward mastery. Every decision echoes across all three layers — from the theatre map to the campaign front to the dogfight above it.

---

## Core Design Principles

- **Abstract over simulate** — the feel of systems matters more than their accuracy
- **Every layer informs every other** — air actions affect ground rolls; ground outcomes affect theatre resources
- **3 minutes is the atomic campaign unit** — tight enough for a session, chainable into a 1–2 hour theatre campaign
- **Decisions have faces** — pilots have names, opponents have personalities, losses have weight
- **Three layers, one pulse** — the game has a consistent rhythm across all scales
- **Scarcity felt through resistance** — constraints communicated through disabled controls, not counters

---

## Setting

**World War I, 1914–1918**

The first great air war. Open cockpits, jamming guns, drifting bombs, ace culture, and a single grinding Western Front. Historically underused in modern games. The era's constraints are gameplay-rich and its aesthetic is distinctive.

Aircraft types: fighters and bombers only — one of each per side, keeping the design focused on strategy over aircraft encyclopaedia.

---

## Three-Layer Architecture

### Layer 1 — The Theatre (Turn-Based)
*The war room. You are a commander.*

### Layer 2 — The Campaign (Real-Time with Pulse)
*The battlefield. You are a general.*

### Layer 3 — The Action (Real-Time, Optional)
*The sky. You are a squadron commander.*

Transitioning between layers is a **zoom** — push in to get closer to the war, pull out to rise above it. The hand-drawn theatre map dissolves into tabletop campaign terrain; the tabletop terrain dissolves into newsreel sky. Zooming back out reverses the journey. No menus, no loading screens — just scale.

---

## Layer 1: The Theatre

### Overview
A large strategic map divided into sectors. The player manages the entire war effort across all sectors, personally fighting in one campaign per turn while the rest resolve automatically.

### Visual Style
**Hand-drawn period map** — cartographic illustration in the style of WWI-era staff maps. Wooden blocks represent force concentrations and front lines. Warm parchment tones, ink lines, period typography. Feels like a war room table in a French château.

### Resources
Three resources, generated by sector and allocated by the player each turn. The same three resources appear at the campaign level — the player learns the model once and applies it at both scales.

| Resource | Generated By | Governs |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Manpower** | Population centers | Front line resilience, pilot replacement rate (40% roll weight) |
| **Equipment** | Factories | Aircraft availability, combat hitting power (30% roll weight) |
| **Food** | Farmland | Sustained effectiveness across the full campaign (30% roll weight) |

**Allocation mechanic:** Each sector generates a baseline of all three resources with a small surplus. The player distributes surplus freely — or cannibalizes quiet sectors to supercharge active ones.

### Turn Structure
1. Review theatre map — check front positions, resource levels, intelligence picture
2. Allocate resources across sectors
3. Choose which sector to personally fight in this turn
4. All other sectors resolve automatically (weighted dice rolls)
5. Resolve campaign in chosen sector
6. Theatre map updates based on campaign outcomes and automatic resolutions

### Win Conditions (Theatre Level)
- **Capital capture** — take the enemy capital city
- **Industrial strangulation** — destroy enough enemy productive capacity that they can no longer sustain the war
- **Provisional capital rule** — if a capital falls, a dice roll determines whether the enemy re-establishes one elsewhere. The war may not be over. Finish the job.

### Campaign → Theatre Translation
- **Margin of victory** → determines how far the front moves in that sector
- **Local capital bonus** → resource windfall for next theatre turn, allocated freely by the player
- Narrow win: small front nudge. Dominant victory: significant territorial lunge. Loss: front retreats proportionally.

## Layer 2: The Campaign

### Overview
A single sector battle lasting **3 minutes**. The player allocates resources along the front, holds back reserves, deploys air actions, and responds to a living front line that resolves on a regular pulse. Zooming into action sequences is optional — the AI handles them if the player stays at map level, but at a disadvantage.

### Visual Style
**Tabletop terrain recreation** — a physical scale model aesthetic. Textured ground, rendered terrain features, front lines that look real rather than abstract. The front leaves devastation behind it as it moves — scorched earth, cratered ground. Animation intensity reflects combat intensity: a segment under heavy pressure churns visibly; a quiet segment is still.

### The Front Line
The front is divided into **6 segments**, each named after a historical WWI location. Each segment is an independent roll, weighted by its allocated resources.

**Segment roll weights:**
- Manpower: 40% of player strength
- Equipment: 30%
- Food: 30%
- Air bonus: additive on top (see Air Actions)

**The air-ground connection:** Air actions and front line rolls share the same clock. A dogfight or bombing run active over a segment directly boosts that segment's roll weight. Air actions are not separate — they are front events viewed from a different scale.

### Pulse Structure

The campaign runs on two rhythms:

| Pulse Type | Frequency | Movement | Air Bonus |
|------------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Sub-pulse** | Every 30 seconds | ±1 per segment | Applies but persists |
| **⚡ Major Push** | Every 3rd sub-pulse (90s) | ±1–3 per segment | Applies then resets |

**6 sub-pulses total = 3 minute campaign.** Major Pushes occur at pulses 3 and 6. A visible countdown and colour change (purple) signal when a Major Push is imminent.

The sub-pulse/Major Push rhythm creates a natural loop: probing skirmishes build pressure, then a decisive push resolves the accumulated tension. The Major Push is when outcomes are largest and attrition is highest.

### Resource Pool
One resource pool at the campaign level. No separate counter — scarcity is communicated through resistance (buttons stop working when the pool is empty).

**Starting values (prototype-validated):**
- Total per resource: 18
- Default per segment: 2 (× 6 segments = 12 allocated)
- Default reserves: 6
- Free to reallocate: 0 — player starts at a balanced state and must make trade-offs

### Reserves
The player holds back resources before the campaign starts. During battle, reserves can be deployed to any segment — exploiting momentum or shoring up a collapse. Depleting reserves entirely is a risk: late-campaign crises have no backstop.

### Pre-Campaign Allocation
A resource legend explains each resource's role and roll weight before the campaign starts. The legend disappears once the campaign begins. Segment cards show per-resource mini bars (colour-coded: green/amber/red) and a composite strength-vs-enemy meter throughout.

### Feedback Systems (validated in prototype)
After each pulse, each segment shows:
- Result indicator (▲ advance / ● hold / ▼ retreat) with magnitude
- Deciding factor in small italic text ("superior firepower" / "supply lines failing")
- Active air bonus badge ("✈ +1.5 active")
- Strength percentage bar (green > 55%, amber 45–55%, red < 45%)

The dispatch log shows:
- Summary (X adv · Y hold · Z ret)
- The two most dramatic segment outcomes with deciding factor
- Air action outcomes per segment ("✈ Dogfight supported the advance" / "💣 Bombing active — still lost ground")
- Aircraft losses in red with ✕ marker

### Campaign Scoring
At the end of 3 minutes:
- Points for net territory held (position × 12)
- Points per segment in positive territory (× 8)
- Bonus for capturing the local capital

Score feeds into theatre layer translation (front movement + potential resource windfall).

### Intelligence & Reconnaissance
Reconned targets receive a bombing effectiveness bonus. Intelligence persists for **5 theatre turns** then degrades — visually represented as reconnaissance photos that yellow and fade.

**Enemy recon is visible.** When an enemy recon plane enters your territory you see it and can scramble fighters to intercept. Leaving it alone gives the enemy a bombing bonus. Scrambling costs a fighter sortie — and the enemy may have sent escort fighters.

## Layer 3: The Action

### Overview
Three action types, all optional. If the player doesn't zoom in, the AI resolves automatically at a disadvantage. The player can zoom out mid-action; the action continues without them.

**Air actions persist until the next Major Push** — not just one sub-pulse. This gives the player the full 90-second window between Major Pushes to zoom in, play out the dogfight or bombing run, and return to the campaign map before the big roll.

### Visual Style
**Colorized period newsreel** — slightly desaturated base with hand-tinted colors, subtle film grain, vignette edges. Planes feel like archival footage. Stylized enough to read clearly as gameplay; atmospheric enough to feel transporting. Not photorealistic — this is history as it was *remembered*, not as it was.

### Aircraft Pools
Each action type draws from a finite pool of aircraft. Losses are permanent for the campaign. When a pool hits zero that action type becomes unavailable.

| Type | Starting Pool | Attrition (sub-pulse) | Attrition (Major Push) |
|------|--------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| ✈ Fighters | 4 | ~22% per active action | ~38% |
| 💣 Bombers | 3 | ~15% | ~28% |
| 📷 Recon | 3 | ~10% | ~20% |

Aircraft losses appear in the dispatch log immediately. A depleted pool greys out that action type's button. Future versions will show named pilots and individual aircraft — for now the pool number is the abstraction.

### Action Type 1: Dogfight

**God-mode squadron command** — the player directs pilots tactically but does not fly a plane. Pilots fly and shoot autonomously; the player issues orders.

**Example orders:**
- Flank left / Flank right
- Dive on them (speed advantage, altitude cost)
- Protect the bombers (escort mode)
- Focus fire on lead plane
- Break and scatter (defensive)
- Disengage and return

Pilot skill (medals) determines how crisply orders are executed. A green recruit may fumble a flanking maneuver. A six-medal ace executes it precisely and adds their own initiative.

Air bonus: **+1.5** to segment strength for the duration. Highest attrition risk.

### Action Type 2: Bombing Run

The player directs bombers over a target area, choosing approach angle to avoid flak, selecting which targets to hit based on recon intelligence. Flak causes damage and disrupts the run.

**Bombing effectiveness is modified by:**
- Whether the target has been reconned (and how recently)
- Approach angle (avoiding flak concentrations)
- Fighter escort (or lack thereof)
- Equipment resource level

Partial destruction is meaningful — destroying half a factory reduces its output by 50%. Targets repair over time.

Air bonus: **+1.2** to segment strength. Medium attrition risk.

### Action Type 3: Reconnaissance Run

Fighters fly a recon route over enemy territory, gathering intelligence that persists for 5 turns. No offensive capability — pilots are exposed with no way to fight back.

**Risk without reward:** Recon pilots gain no kills, no medals. Lost on a recon mission feels particularly costly — they weren't fighting back.

Air bonus: **+0.8** to segment strength. Lowest attrition risk.

## Pilots & Progression

### The Roster System
- **15 followed pilots** — named, tracked, progressing
- **Up to 10** can be taken into any campaign
- **5 remain on the bench** — safe but not progressing while you're elsewhere
- **Green recruits** fill out rosters in sectors you're not personally fighting in

### Progression
Pilots earn **medals** through kills and successful missions (up to 6). Medals represent experience and combat ability — a six-medal ace executes orders precisely and adds their own initiative. Bombers have names too (named aircraft, period-appropriate).

### Permadeath
- Followed pilots (active 10) die through direct campaign action — fair, player-caused
- Bench pilots (the 5 left behind) are exposed to sector collapse losses — any of the 15, regardless of medals, can die if their sector collapses badly enough while you're elsewhere
- Green recruits in unattended sectors die in collapses, draining the replacement pipeline
- A pilot lost on recon — fighting nothing, dying anyway — is the cruelest loss

### The Bench Decision
Choosing which 5 to leave behind is a risk decision, not a storage decision. "I need you here more than there" — with real consequences if that sector collapses.

### Visual Representation (Deferred)
Named pilots and individual aircraft will eventually have explicit visual indicators — portrait icons, individual loss notifications. The current prototype abstracts this to pool numbers, which is the right starting point. Visual identity comes once the mechanical layer is validated.

## AI Opponents

### Three Archetype Pools (Randomly Combined)

**Theatre Commander** — governs resource allocation and sector targeting between turns
- Aggressive (overcommits to breakthrough sectors)
- Conservative (spreads evenly, rarely gambles)
- Opportunist (targets your weakest sector)
- Attritional (focuses on your industrial base)

**Campaign General** — governs front resource allocation and in-campaign response
- Flanker
- Hammer (straight through center)
- Defender
- Blitzer (goes hard early, fades late)

**Ace** — governs dogfight and air tactical behavior
- Aggressive
- Defensive
- Escort-focused
- Hunter (ignores bombers, targets your fighters and recon planes)

### Difficulty & Combination Logic
- **Easy** — combinations weighted toward *contradictory* archetypes (an Aggressive theatre commander starving the sectors his Defender general needs)
- **Hard** — combinations weighted toward *complementary* archetypes (Attritional commander + Hammer general + Hunter ace = systematic, grinding, coherent pressure)

### Visibility Toggle
Player can choose to see opponent archetypes or keep them hidden. Hidden + Hard is the game's most demanding mode — you must discover the AI's coherent strategy through play.

### Opponent Voice
Each archetype has a quoted philosophy shown before the campaign. "I go for the throat and damn the flanks" communicates more than "Aggressive archetype." Opponents feel like adversaries, not settings.

## Controls (iPad-First)

### Strategic & Campaign Map
- **Touch** — tap to select, drag to assign flight paths and resource allocation, pinch to zoom between layers
- Designed primarily for touch; all map interactions are tap/drag native

### Action Layer
- **Bluetooth keyboard** — primary control method, full tactical order set
- **Touch fallback** — on-screen command palette for touch-only players; simplified but complete
- The god-mode command structure (issuing orders rather than flying) makes touch viable in a way direct flight control never would be

### Universal
- Variable game speed — slow down when overwhelmed, speed up in quiet moments
- Pause available in action layer
- Resource allocation time-stops the campaign clock
- Zoom transition is always player-initiated

## Prototype Status

### Campaign Layer — ✅ Validated
The core front-line pulse mechanic has been prototyped and tested. The following values are considered starting points for the real implementation:

- Sub-pulse interval: 30 seconds
- Campaign length: 6 sub-pulses (3 minutes)
- Major Push: every 3rd sub-pulse
- Resources total: 18 per type
- Default segment allocation: 2 per segment
- Default reserves: 6 per type
- Aircraft pools: Fighters 4 / Bombers 3 / Recon 3
- Position range: ±8

### Remaining to Prototype
1. **Theatre map layer** — sector map, wooden block UI, turn resolution
2. **Zoom transition** — hand-drawn → tabletop → newsreel
3. **Dogfight god-mode** — squadron command UI, order system
4. **Bombing run** — approach angle, flak, target selection
5. **Named pilot system** — roster management, visual identity
6. **AI opponent logic** — archetype behaviours at each layer

## Open Questions

1. **Multiplayer fork** — architecture should allow a human player to slot in where the AI theatre commander sits. Design AI systems with clean interfaces.
2. **Campaign editor** — the original's map editor was a major replayability feature. Consider for post-launch.
3. **Recruit pool size** — how many recruits fill out an unattended sector roster? TBD.
4. **Intelligence degradation curve** — linear decay over 5 turns, or stepped? TBD.
5. **Attrition rate calibration** — prototype rates feel approximately right but need more playtesting across full campaigns.
6. **Major Push drama** — does it feel sufficiently different from sub-pulses in practice? Worth testing with actual dogfight layer running simultaneously.
